One of the most important steps a patient can take before booking cosmetic surgery abroad is verifying that the clinic holds genuine, internationally recognised accreditation. This single piece of due diligence provides significant assurance that the facility meets established standards of safety, governance, and patient care.

Yet many patients skip this step entirely — or rely on claims made in clinic marketing materials without independent verification. This guide explains what accreditation means, which ones matter, and how to check for yourself.

## What Is International Clinic Accreditation?

Accreditation is a formal recognition process in which an independent body assesses a healthcare facility against a set of published standards. When a clinic or hospital achieves accreditation, it has demonstrated — through on-site inspection and documentation review — that it meets those standards at the time of assessment.

Accreditation is not a one-time award. It requires ongoing compliance and periodic renewal. Accredited facilities are subject to follow-up assessments and can lose their status if standards slip.

## The Gold Standard: JCI Accreditation

Joint Commission International (JCI) is the most widely recognised international healthcare accreditation body. JCI accreditation is the international extension of The Joint Commission, which accredits healthcare organisations in the United States, and applies the same rigorous standards globally.

JCI-accredited hospitals in Turkey, Thailand, or elsewhere have been assessed across more than 1,200 measurable standards covering patient care, safety protocols, infection control, facility management, governance, and staff qualifications. Achieving and maintaining JCI accreditation is demanding — and that is precisely what makes it meaningful.

**To verify JCI accreditation:** Visit the official JCI website at jointcommissioninternational.org and use the "Find an Accredited Organisation" tool. Search by country and organisation name. If the clinic appears in the results with a current accreditation status, it's legitimate. If it doesn't appear, or appears with an expired status, be cautious.

## Other Recognised Accreditation Standards

JCI is not the only credible accreditation, but it is the most universally recognised. Others worth knowing about include:

**ISO 9001 certification** — an international standard for quality management systems. While less specific to healthcare than JCI, ISO 9001 certification indicates that the organisation operates systematic quality control processes. It is typically held alongside clinical accreditations.

**ISAPS membership** — the International Society of Aesthetic Plastic Surgery is a professional association for plastic surgeons. While membership doesn't constitute accreditation of the facility, it indicates that individual surgeons are engaged with international professional standards.

**Temos International Healthcare Accreditation** — a Germany-based accreditation body specifically focused on medical tourism facilities. It assesses clinics on quality, patient safety, and the specific requirements of international patients.

## Red Flags to Watch For

Certain patterns in clinic communications should make you more vigilant about verifying accreditation:

- Vague references to "internationally recognised standards" without naming a specific body
- Accreditation logos on websites that cannot be verified through the issuing body's own portal
- Claims of accreditation from bodies you cannot find through independent searching
- Reluctance to provide accreditation certificate numbers that would allow independent verification

None of these patterns is definitive evidence of fraud, but each warrants deeper investigation before any commitment is made.

## Verifying Surgeon Credentials

Beyond facility accreditation, verifying the credentials of the specific surgeon who will perform your procedure is equally important. In Turkey, surgeons in plastic and reconstructive surgery are regulated by the Turkish Medical Association and the Turkish Society of Plastic, Reconstructive, and Aesthetic Surgery (TPCD).

Ask for your surgeon's registration number and verify it through official channels. Ask which university they graduated from and where they completed their specialty training. If a surgeon trained abroad (in Germany, France, the US, or elsewhere), ask for documentation of that training.
